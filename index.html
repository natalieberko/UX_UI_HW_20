NSA.gov

Responsive Redesign of the National Security Agency Website

Overview

Many government agencies find it challenging to build website experiences where information is organized around user needs rather than agency needs. For many of these government websites, the UI design is outdated and the content is very information heavy.

I was tasked with a responsive redesign of the information architecture, interface, branding, and assets of the current NSA website in order to improve its usability.

Background

The National Security Agency (NSA) is an intelligence agency of the U.S. Department of Defense. The NSA leads the U.S. Government in cryptology, the science of making and breaking codes, in order to gain a decision advantage for the nation and our allies under all circumstances. The Central Security Service (CSS), part of NSA, provides cryptologic support, knowledge, and assistance to the military cryptologic community. CSS coordinates and develops policy and guidance on missions of NSA/CSS to ensure military integration.

Source: intel.gov

What's wrong the NSA website?

Heuristic Evaluation

I executed a heuristic evaluation of the pages Hank visits, that were defined in his user path, as a way to identify usability problems in the website’s current user interface. I analyzed the appearance, accessibility, content, and functionality of the website.

Tools: Adobe XD

MAIN PROBLEMS:
  • The terminology is confusing.

    • It is jargon-heavy with words that the average person may not understand, making the pages difficult to scan.

  • There are too many external links.

    • The user is sent to different websites in order to achieve a task, primarily when looking for career information and opportunities.

  • The website is not easy to navigate.

    • Getting around the site is not easy and the user has to do a lot of guesswork. One reason is the confusing terminology, but also the submenus aren’t categorized in any particular way and feel overwhelming to browse through. It’s hard to get to know the site and remember where everything is.

Responsive Redesign

From the usability testing and heuristic evaluation, I realized that the biggest pain point, and perhaps the root of all pain points, was the present navigation.

What isn't working?

Heuristic Evaluation

I executed a heuristic evaluation of the pages Hank visits, that were defined in his user path, as a way to identify usability problems in the website’s current user interface. I analyzed the appearance, accessibility, content, and functionality of the website.

Tools: Adobe XD

MAIN PROBLEMS:

    Overcrowded menus.

    Submenus and their submenus need to be grouped, sorted, and relabeled. There are too many lists to go through without enough context as to what each list item means. Context can be developed and communicated better by changing the page layout from just words and lists to actual sections with appropriate headings and descriptions.

    Footer information isn't helpful.

    Need to re-evaluate which information to put in the footer. Information should be quick access for users.

    The homepage content doesn't help users achieve their goals.

    The sections and headings aren’t all clearly defined and some of the terminology may be confusing to most people. There isn’t really a flow to the homepage. The flow of the homepage should reflect the flow of the navigation menu so users can learn the website and find what they are looking for more easily.

    Participants did not have faith in the contact forms.

    Most expressed they do not believe they will receive a timely reply, if at all.

    Information logic of the main navigation is confusing.

    Along with the homepage, the main nav seems to be lacking a flow. How do the submenus relate to their menu option and to each other? They don’t.

How might we address some of these problems?

New Website Navigation

I began to design a new navigation with these goals in mind:

    Reduce the number of times the user has to leave the website.

    For example, keep careers nested within the NSA website.

    Make contact information easily available.

    Eliminate the guesswork.

    Use terminology that is understandable by everyone to improve usability.

    Reduce the number of submenus.

    Chunk the content into labels that everyone can understand to improve usability.

Mid-Fidelity Responsive Solutions

I wireframed responsive design solutions for both desktop and mobile.

NEW NAVIGATION MENU

I chose the hamburger menu for its responsiveness but also because I noticed it is commonly used in other intelligence agencies websites so I followed this trend. I completely eliminated the overcrowded submenus and designed a clear and simplified navigation menu.

FOOTER WITH SUBMENUS

The footer contains all of the menu options with the sections users will find in each page to make it easier for the user to navigate . There is also a Contact NSA button available in the footer.

HOMEPAGE

I went for a captivating homepage that gives the user a general idea of who the NSA is. I included sections that the user might navigate to with appropriate calls to action. The homepage also reflects the main navigation options.

CAREERS PAGE

I included a careers page that is nested within the website so users aren’t sent to an external page.

MORE DEVELOPED PAGES

For my solution to the overcrowded submenus, I organized the content in 2nd and 3rd level pages with sections. This way, users aren’t exposed to too much information yet they can choose to learn more if they wish. I also made sure to include breadcrumbs so users know where they are within the site.

Tools: Adobe XD

Visual Design and Branding

How might we portray the NSA brand?

Since I’m designing for a government website, I wanted to keep the design simple and modern and incorporate elements that evoke trust and reliability. The hues of blue inspire these feelings. I also wanted to incorporate images that show the NSA’s values. For example, diversity is an important value of the NSA, so include images that portray a diverse representation of people.

Testing the Responsive Redesign

How do users feel navigating through the new design?

Now it’s time to validate my solutions. I conducted usability tests with these questions in mind:

    Is the navigation clear and easy to use?

    Do users feel confident that they will find the information they are looking for with its present organization?

    Do users get a general idea of who the NSA is from the homepage?

    Do the sections on the homepage reflect the information people seek when visiting the NSA website?

7 participants were recruited to test the new navigation of the responsive redesign via Zoom.

PROCEDURE:

Scroll through the homepage.

Click on navigation tab.

Explore menu options.

Explain what information you may find for each menu option.

Feedback/ comments about design, content, and organization.

Key Takeaways

    Know your agency. Know your content.

    I spent a lot of time getting to know the current NSA.gov website before I was able to begin the responsive redesign. I realized I had to really understand the content before I reorganized it. In addition, I struggled to get an understanding of who the NSA is so I spent a lot of time doing research about the agency in order to get a true sense of the brand I wanted to convey.

    The heuristic evaluation and navigation annotations really helped me realize the changes I needed to make in the redesign.

    Working with components.

    This is the first time I’ve worked with components and they truly are a game changer. They saved me a lot of time and I felt the whole design process went a lot smoother. I was able to keep my designs and iterations consistent and make changes very easily.

    The future of this redesign.

    I am looking forward to continue working on this redesign. Based on the feedback I received from user tests, I intend to:

    • Make sure to clearly define everything so users aren’t confused. For example, users weren’t sure what CSS meant (it means Central Security Services).
    • Refine the sitemap and navigation to include menu options that flow better.
    • Design the contact form with drop down menus
    • Use images to better communicate to the user what they may expect to find in that section.
    • Design more responsive pages across more modalities, such as tablet.
    • Include warning pop up messages whenever users must leave the site.
